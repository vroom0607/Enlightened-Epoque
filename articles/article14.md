### **Diagnosing Your Skin Type**
Knowing our skin type is the foundation of any successful skincare routine. Dermatologists typically categorize skin into five main types: normal, dry, oily, combination, and sensitive (American Academy of Dermatology Association).

Here’s a simple way to find out what type your skin falls in: Wash your face with a gentle, non-stripping (water-based!) cleanser and avoid putting anything else on for an hour after. Observe how your skin feels, or if you really want to make sure, grab a few sheets of blotting paper and press one on your forehead, cheeks, nose, and chin to see which part of your face produces oil. **Match your results to these overarching types below:**

- **Dry Skin:** Skin feels tight or flaky; little or no oil is observed on the blotting paper.

- **Oily Skin:** Skin appears shiny, especially in the T-zone (forehead, nose, and chin); lots of oil on the blotting paper.

- **Combination Skin:** Skin shows oiliness in some areas (especially the T-zone) and dryness in others; varied results on the blotting papers.

- **Sensitive Skin:** Skin may sting, burn, or react easily; some oil will appear on blotting papers.

- **Normal Skin:** Skin feels balanced, neither too dry nor too oily; the blotting papers will show a healthy variation of oil. 

According to the Cleveland Clinic, understanding how your skin behaves throughout the day is often more accurate than relying on how it looks immediately after washing.

### **Tips for Each Skin Type**
**Dry Skin**

This skin type doesn’t come equipped with sufficient moisture that the oil glands on the face produce, resulting in a compromised skin barrier. To combat this, focus on barrier repair and maintenance through hydrating products. Dermatologists recommend using gentle, fragrance-free cleansers and rich moisturizers containing ingredients like ceramides, glycerin, and hyaluronic acid (AAD). Don’t fall for the trendy exfoliators! If congestion is present, opt for gentle chemical exfoliating acids rather than their harsh physical counterparts. 

**Oily Skin**

On the opposite of the spectrum, oily skin often suffers from an excess of sebum produced by the oil glands. Now, it may be tempting to dry out every pore if treating dry skin was through hydration right? Wrong! Stripping the natural moisture from the face will only result in the oil glands working overtime (well, more than they already do). Thus, the key still remains to prioritize barrier health, with a focus on oil regulation and gentle cleansing. Opt for thinner, gel-like moisturizers rather than thick, rich creams. Non-comedogenic products, lightweight moisturizers, and ingredients like salicylic acid or niacinamide can help control oil without disrupting the skin barrier (Mayo Clinic).

**Combination Skin**

This is the category most people fall into. Places around the T-zone may feel shiny but other spots around the outer portions of the face might feel tight or stripped. Once again, barrier health, barrier health, barrier health!! Opt for hydrating products that are lighter in texture, the best of both dry and oily skin worlds, as dermatologists say over-treating one area can worsen imbalance elsewhere, making moderation key (Cleveland Clinic). Taking proper care of this skin type usually requires some trial and error, be patient!

**Sensitive Skin**

Skin is considered sensitive if irritation, redness, or other reactions frequently occur. Barrier health is crucial, if not the most important aspect of a sensitive skincare routine. The ability for this organ to bounce back from the wear and tear of everyday life lies in the wellbeing of the skin barrier. Another important factor is to minimize irritants. Experts recommend avoiding fragrance and limiting active ingredients. Keeping the routine as short and simple as possible helps reduce irritation and strengthen the skin’s natural defenses (AAD).

### **Closing Thoughts**
Don’t be fooled by clever marketing or pretty bottles! The world of skincare is vast and extensive but with real, science-backed knowledge, understanding what each complexion needs to achieve radiance feels much more manageable. Skincare doesn’t have to be complicated to be effective. By discovering our skin types, we can then cater a routine that reflects what we need, not what we see on the for you page. As dermatologists consistently emphasize, consistency and simplicity are often more powerful than chasing the latest trend.
