### **The Economics Behind Calories**
Unhealthy food is cheap, and that's why the majority of Americans’ health is on the steady decline. Highly-processed food is engineered to be addicting and shelf-stable while whole, nutritious food spoils faster and has consistently been on the upcharge in the 20th century. In fact, research from Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health found that healthier diets cost about $1.50 more per day than less healthy ones (Rao et al.). Though this difference seems small, it adds up quickly. Every penny counts, especially for struggling families. Over time, choosing cheaper, processed foods becomes the more financially sustainable option, even if it risks long-term health.

### **Food Deserts**
Sometimes, it's not just the cost of food that prevents healthy living, it’s also about accessibility. Many communities that lie far away from large cities lack access to full grocery stores and instead rely on convenience stores or fast-food chains. These places are referred to as food deserts, because people living in these areas are isolated from fresh food options. Studies have shown they face reduced access to affordable fruits, vegetables, and whole foods, pushing them toward processed alternatives (USDA).

### **Convenience Culture**
Another aspect to consider is time. Cooking at home from scratch requires hours that are simply unattainable for many working corporate jobs, single parents juggling their children’s activities, or college students drowning in homework and odd jobs. 

That's where fast food saves the day. Or so we think. They’re designed for speed and ease, making them appear practical for the lifestyles listed above. However, they’re packed with excessive amounts of added sugar and unhealthy fats, slowly deteriorating health over time. 

### **The Power of Marketing**
The main problem behind all this is marketing. Fast food companies don’t just sell food, they sell deals and volume. All the “more for less” steals we think we gain only subtly encourage us to consume more than what our bodies need to function. Slowly, American portions have only grown in size, while nutrients have rapidly dwindled. Now, many of our expectations surrounding food have changed as it becomes harder for us to recognize what a balanced portion actually looks like. 

### **What You Can Do**
1. **Practice Mindful Portions:** Assess the environment around you. Are there bargains plastered all over the walls? Chances are, a “value deal” added two unnecessary burgers to your plate.

2. **Remain Present:** Reduce the amount of distractions surrounding you. Take a break from scrolling and really sit and enjoy your food! This will make it much easier to pay attention to your biological fullness cues that the fast food industry tries so hard to hijack.

3. **Think Ahead:** “Meal prepping” is often a term associated with this idea. Take some time during Sundays to prepare healthy and nourishing dishes for the week! And hey, you only have to clean up once. Win-win!

4. **Build Consistent Habits:** Ditch the “all-or-nothing” mindset. Give yourself some grace and get back up on the horse. Progress doesn’t happen in one day; think about all the time you have to live your life to the fullest.

### **Closing Thoughts**
America’s rising obesity rate is influenced by a “complex interplay of environmental, economic, and social factors,” rather than just personal choices or failures (Harvard Health Publishing). As a whole, society consistently makes unhealthy food the most convenient (and sometimes the only) option. This isn’t an epidemic a vaccine can cure: it’s a problem that must be recognized and regulated. 

Addressing this issue allows us to make more informed decisions and possibly begin to advocate for change. We all must play our part to continually create a healthier environment for everyone.
